### Non-inferiority of digitally assisted outpatient rehabilitation in patients with back pain: 12-month follow-up of a randomized controlled trial
- Year: 2026
- Authors: Richard Albers, Stella Lemke, David Fauser, F. Schaffer, Sebastian Knapp, Gert Krischak, Matthias Bethge
- Source: Journal of Rehabilitation Medicine
- DOI: https://doi.org/10.2340/jrm.v58.44366
- OpenAlex: https://openalex.org/W7161848614
- URL: https://doi.org/10.2340/jrm.v58.44366
- Keywords: Rehabilitation; Physical therapy; Medicine; Randomized controlled trial; Confidence interval
- Thesis angle: Use this paper as evidence for a thesis conversation; refine the angle with the supervisor.
- Abstract: OBJECTIVE: To examine the non-inferiority of digitally assisted, multimodal rehabilitation that utilizes a digital version of a standardized back school (intervention group) compared with a rehabilitation program applying the back school conventionally in person (control group). DESIGN: Nonblinded, randomized, controlled, non-inferiority trial with 12-month follow-up after the end of rehabilitation. SUBJECTS/PATIENTS: Adults aged 18-65 years with back pain. METHODS: The back school was part of a 3-week rehabilitation program along with other treatments in accordance with the therapy standards. Eight outpatient rehabilitation centers conducted the rehabilitation program. Self-reported pain self-efficacy (10-60 points) was the primary outcome. Non-inferiority was established if the lower limit of the one-sided 95% confidence interval was greater than -4 points for pain self-efficacy. RESULTS: A total of 157 participants (55.3%) completed the 12-month follow-up. Multiple imputation of missing data allowed for inclusion of 270 participants in the analysis (intervention group: n = 127, control group: n = 143). Our primary adjusted intention- to-treat analysis demonstrated that digitally assisted rehabilitation was non-inferior to conventional rehabilitation at the 12-month follow-up (b = 0.48; 95% CI = -3.09 to ). CONCLUSION: This study supports the hypothesis that digitally assisted rehabilitation is a viable alternative to in-person rehabilitation for patients with back pain.
