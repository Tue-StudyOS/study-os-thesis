# Paper Search Rubric

## Source Priority

Prefer these sources, in order:

1. Publisher, conference, journal, or proceedings pages.
2. arXiv, OpenReview, HAL, institutional repositories, or author pages.
3. OpenAlex, Semantic Scholar, DBLP, Crossref, Google Scholar snippets, or library indexes.
4. Lab news pages and project pages.
5. Blog posts or social posts only as pointers to primary sources.

## Recency

Default to the last 3 years for fast-moving areas such as machine learning, robotics, NLP, computer vision, and data science.

Use a broader window for theory, systems, HCI, education, or niche domains when recent results are sparse. If an older paper is foundational, include it but label it as foundational rather than recent.

## Ranking Criteria

Rank papers by:

- Match to the student's stated interests and skills.
- Usefulness for forming a thesis question.
- Evidence quality and source traceability.
- Recency relative to the field.
- Connection to possible local chairs or supervisors.

## Summary Style

Keep summaries practical:

- What problem does the paper attack?
- What methods or data does it use?
- What could a student learn or extend?
- What background would help before contacting a chair?

Avoid overclaiming. If only the title and abstract are available, say that the assessment is based on title and abstract.
